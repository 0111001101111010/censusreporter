Selected Census Bureau Documentation
  • Geographic Terms and Concepts - Place

Despite the centrality of geography to Census data, many key concepts are not centrally defined. Instead, each data release documents the subsets of geographic metadata that are relevant to that release. Fortunately, the metadata is consistent from release to release—it's just sometimes hard to find a single source that lays out all of the details.

Summary Levels

All data in the American Community Survey is aggregated to a specific summary level. Summary levels are classes of geographies, or shapes on a map. All of the geographies in a summary level are independent—they never overlap each other. But sometimes they don't fall neatly inside larger summary levels.

These are the fundamental summary levels of the Census:

010
The United States
040
States
050
Counties
140
Census Tracts

For each of these, the next level down is a perfect subdivision—that is, all of the "parent" geography is covered by the "children." As always, there are subtle exceptions: Puerto Rico is a 040 summary level, but it is not technically part of the 010 (United States). And, while Washington, DC is not a state, it is counted in 040, and it is part of 010. And while there is no true county government for DC, there is a record for it in the 050 summary level, to maintain this consistency.

Places

Reporters will often want numbers for the cities and towns in their coverage area. This can be more complicated than you might first believe.

Incorporated cities, towns, and villages are part of the 160 summary level, generically described as places The 160 level also includes "Census designated places," or CDPs, which provide data for unincorporated areas which are commonly recognized by people who live in or near them. Place boundaries can divide census tracts and cross county borders, but a place is always contained in a single state.

There is no clearly designated "official" list of Census summary levels, but there is in the American FactFinder help documentation. The Missouri Census Data Center created a list designed for use with SAS statistical software. (Even if you don't use SAS, it's easy to read and may come in handy sometimes.) It's part of their own extensive documentation about Census summary levels.
